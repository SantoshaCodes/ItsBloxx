5 Plumbing Problems You Should Never Ignore

RC
Robert Chen
Jan 20, 2026 Â· 7 min read

Some plumbing issues might seem minor at first glance, but ignoring them can lead to costly damage and even health hazards. A dripping faucet, for example, wastes thousands of gallons of water per year and often signals worn-out internal components that will only get worse. Slow drains are another red flag -- while a single slow drain might just need a simple cleaning, multiple slow drains suggest a deeper problem in your main sewer line. Running toilets, low water pressure, and discolored water are also warning signs that demand professional attention. Low water pressure could indicate pipe corrosion or hidden leaks behind walls, while brown or yellow water may signal aging galvanized pipes that are rusting from the inside. The key takeaway: addressing small problems early saves you from emergency repairs later.

How to Prevent Frozen Pipes This Winter

MG
Maria Gonzalez
Jan 12, 2026 Â· 6 min read

Frozen pipes are one of the most common and destructive winter plumbing emergencies in the Pacific Northwest. When water freezes inside a pipe, it expands with enough force to crack copper, PVC, and even steel pipes, potentially causing thousands of dollars in water damage when the ice thaws. Prevention starts with insulation: wrap exposed pipes in unheated areas like basements, crawl spaces, attics, and garages with foam pipe insulation or heating tape. On especially cold nights, let faucets drip slightly to keep water moving through the system. Open cabinet doors under kitchen and bathroom sinks to let warm air reach the pipes. Keep your thermostat set to at least 55 degrees Fahrenheit, even when you are away.

Water Heater Repair vs. Replacement: How to Decide

DP
David Park
Jan 5, 2026 Â· 8 min read

When your water heater starts acting up, the big question is whether to repair or replace it. Several factors should guide your decision. First, consider the age of your unit: most tank water heaters last 8 to 12 years, while tankless models can last 15 to 20 years. If your unit is nearing the end of its expected lifespan, replacement is usually the smarter investment. Second, look at repair costs: a good rule of thumb is that if the repair costs more than 50 percent of a new unit, replacement makes more financial sense. Third, consider energy efficiency -- newer models are significantly more efficient, which can lower your monthly utility bills by 10 to 20 percent. Signs that replacement is imminent include rusty water coming from hot taps, rumbling noises, and visible corrosion on the tank.
