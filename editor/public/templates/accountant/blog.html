5 Tax Deductions Small Businesses Miss Every Year

KC
Katherine Chen, CPA
Jan 20, 2026 Â· 7 min read

Small business owners leave thousands of dollars on the table every tax season simply because they don't know about deductions they're entitled to claim. After reviewing hundreds of returns at Summit Financial Group, we consistently see the same overlooked write-offs. The home office deduction remains one of the most underutilized -- if you use a dedicated space in your home exclusively for business, you can deduct a proportionate share of your rent or mortgage, utilities, and insurance. Vehicle expenses are another common miss; many business owners forget to track mileage for client meetings, bank runs, and supply pickups. Section 179 allows you to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment in the year you buy it rather than depreciating it over time. Professional development costs like conferences, courses, and industry publications are fully deductible. Finally, don't overlook health insurance premiums -- self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of premiums paid for themselves and their families.

When to Hire an Accountant vs. Using Software

DM
David Morales, CPA
Jan 12, 2026 Â· 6 min read

The rise of accounting software like QuickBooks, FreshBooks, and Wave has made it easier than ever for business owners to manage their own books. But there's a critical inflection point where DIY bookkeeping starts costing you more than it saves. If your business has employees, operates in multiple states, or generates more than $250,000 in annual revenue, the complexity of tax law and compliance requirements makes professional guidance essential. Software is excellent for tracking income and expenses, generating invoices, and basic reporting. However, it cannot advise you on entity structuring, tax planning strategies, or help you navigate an IRS audit. The real question isn't whether you can do your own accounting -- it's whether doing it yourself is the highest and best use of your time. Most business owners we work with find that delegating their finances frees up 10-15 hours per month to focus on revenue-generating activities.

Quarterly Tax Planning: A Step-by-Step Guide

SO
Sarah Okonkwo, CPA
Jan 5, 2026 Â· 8 min read

Quarterly tax planning is one of the most powerful tools for reducing your tax burden, yet most business owners only think about taxes once a year -- during filing season. By then, it's too late to implement most strategies. Effective quarterly planning starts with estimating your annual income and calculating estimated tax payments to avoid underpayment penalties. Each quarter, review your profit and loss statement, identify any large upcoming expenses that could be timed strategically, and assess whether you're on track with retirement contributions. Q1 is ideal for reviewing your entity structure and making changes before the bulk of the year's income is earned. Q2 and Q3 are perfect for mid-year adjustments to withholdings and estimated payments. Q4 is your last chance for year-end strategies like equipment purchases, charitable donations, and retirement plan contributions. Working with a CPA quarterly ensures you never miss a deadline and always have a clear picture of your tax liability.
